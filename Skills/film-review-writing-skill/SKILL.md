# Film Review Writing

Use this skill to produce Chinese film reviews that read like careful student or early academic writing: restrained, concrete, film-centered, and supported by specific scenes, shots, sounds, actions, dialogue fragments, and narrative details.

## Core Principles

- Obey the user's explicit assignment requirements first: film title, word count, format, thesis, course theme, competition rubric, citation style, and forbidden content.
- Ground every major claim in observable film evidence. Prefer exact scenes, camera movement, framing, lighting, color, sound, editing, blocking, props, gestures, dialogue fragments, and plot turns.
- Avoid inventing details. If the user has not provided enough film-specific material and the film is unfamiliar, ask for notes or clearly state that a reliable review needs scene details.
- Keep the tone objective, calm, and analytical. Avoid exaggerated praise, slogans, emotional confession, rhetorical questions, exclamation marks, and empty moralizing.
- Write in natural Chinese prose. Vary sentence length, avoid mechanical parallelism, and do not make every paragraph share the same visible template.
- Use film theory lightly. Terms are useful only when they clarify the film, not when they decorate the essay.

## Input Check

Before writing, identify what the user has provided:

- Film title and, if useful, year/director/region.
- Required word count or target length.
- Purpose: course assignment, competition review, short review, long essay, speech draft, or revision.
- Required angle: audiovisual language, narrative, character, theme, genre, realism, ideology, adaptation, etc.
- User's viewing notes or specific scenes.

If key information is missing but the task is still possible, make a conservative assumption and proceed. If the task requires specific film details that are not available, ask for the minimum missing information instead of fabricating.

## Writing Workflow

1. Establish a thesis.
   - State the film's central artistic feature in one restrained sentence.
   - Make the thesis specific enough to guide the whole essay.
   - Avoid generic claims such as "the film is deeply moving" or "it has profound meaning."

2. Select evidence.
   - Choose 2-4 concrete scenes or recurring details before drafting.
   - Cover more than plot summary: include at least two of image, sound, editing, performance, setting, props, dialogue, or rhythm.
   - Tie each evidence point to the thesis.

3. Build the structure.
   - Use a flexible essay shape rather than a rigid visible template.
   - For 800-1200 words: introduction, audiovisual/narrative analysis, character/theme analysis, conclusion.
   - For 1200-2800 words: introduction, audiovisual language, narrative and dramatic structure, character construction, symbols/themes, conclusion.
   - For short reviews under 600 words: thesis, 2-3 evidence clusters, concise judgment.

4. Draft paragraphs.
   - Begin with a clear analytical claim.
   - Add a brief explanation.
   - Bring in concrete film evidence.
   - End by explaining the evidence's function in the film.
   - Do not repeat the same paragraph rhythm too visibly.

5. Revise for naturalness.
   - Remove generic filler, over-neat transitions, and repeated sentence frames.
   - Replace broad labels with specific details from the film.
   - Keep value judgments restrained and justified by evidence.

## Recommended Essay Shape

Use this as a default structure when the user does not provide a required format.

### Opening

- Introduce the film's basic information only as needed: title, year, director, genre, setting, or premise.
- Move quickly from basic information to an analytical thesis.
- Preview the main analytical dimensions without listing them mechanically.

### Audiovisual Language

Discuss how the film uses image and sound to shape meaning. Choose only details that are actually visible or audible in the film:

- Color and lighting: cold/warm contrast, brightness, shadow, texture, atmosphere.
- Framing and camera: close-up, medium shot, long shot, handheld movement, fixed camera, tracking, distance, angle.
- Editing and rhythm: duration, cuts, pauses, repetition, contrast between fast and slow passages.
- Sound: ambient sound, silence, music, speech, noise, sound-image relationship.

### Narrative and Dramatic Construction

Discuss how the story is organized:

- Setup, conflict, turning point, climax, aftermath.
- Linear, fragmented, circular, multi-perspective, or memory-based structure.
- Pacing, suspense, withheld information, repetition, contrast, daily-life detail.
- Genre expectation and whether the film follows, weakens, or breaks it.

### Character Construction

Analyze characters through behavior rather than labels:

- Initial condition: ordinary traits, weakness, limitation, desire, or contradiction.
- Pressure: external conflict, social situation, family relation, work, memory, or moral dilemma.
- Change: visible shifts in action, expression, silence, speech, choice, or relation to others.
- Outcome: whether the character gains clarity, remains trapped, compromises, resists, or changes the audience's understanding.

### Symbols and Themes

Extract symbols naturally from the film instead of forcing interpretation:

- Title, repeated props, locations, weather, colors, songs, rituals, names, gestures, or recurring shots.
- Explain what the symbol does in the film: connect characters, compress conflict, mark time, reveal social context, or deepen theme.
- Keep theme discussion tied to film evidence. Avoid ending in broad inspirational language.

### Conclusion

- Summarize the essay's main findings without copying earlier topic sentences.
- Return to the film's artistic value and human or social meaning.
- Keep the ending calm, precise, and earned.

## Style Rules

- Prefer plain analytical Chinese over ornate prose.
- Avoid: "震撼人心", "发人深省", "淋漓尽致", "引人入胜", "催人泪下", "具有重要现实意义" unless immediately supported by precise film evidence.
- Avoid obvious AI transitions: "首先、其次、最后", "综上所述", "不可否认的是", "值得一提的是" when they make the essay sound templated.
- Avoid excessive parallelism and symmetrical sentence groups.
- Avoid giving a star rating unless the user asks.
- Do not mention "AI rate", "查重", "降重", "模板", or "生成" in the final review.

## Detail Density Standard

For a full essay, each main body paragraph should contain at least two concrete film details. A detail can be:

- A visible image or composition.
- A camera distance or movement.
- A color, lighting condition, or setting.
- A sound, silence, music cue, or voice.
- A character action, gesture, expression, or pause.
- A dialogue fragment or repeated phrase.
- A plot event or dramatic turn.
- A prop, location, costume, or repeated motif.

If a paragraph contains only abstract nouns, revise it.

## Output Modes

When the user asks for a complete review, output only the review unless they request explanation.

When the user asks for improvement or revision, provide:

- A brief diagnosis of the current draft's main problems.
- A revised version or a rewrite plan.
- Optional line-level suggestions when useful.

When the user asks for an outline, provide:

- Thesis.
- Section structure.
- Evidence points to collect or use.
- Suggested paragraph order.

When the user asks for a prompt or reusable rule, provide a clean skill-like instruction set instead of a single-use prompt.

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing, check:

- Does every major claim connect to film-specific evidence?
- Are there any invented or unverifiable details?
- Is the essay more analysis than plot summary?
- Does the conclusion avoid empty uplift?
- Does the prose sound like a careful human essay rather than a rigid template?
